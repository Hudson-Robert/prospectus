An Autoethnographic and Phenomenological Study of Resilience and Higher Education in Post-Incarceration Reintegration

Key Definitions

Post-incarceration reintegration

The complex, ongoing process through which formerly incarcerated individuals navigate the transition from correctional facilities back into community settings, involving practical, psychological, social, and identity-based adjustments that extend beyond immediate reentry needs.

Meaning-making

The active, interpretive process through which individuals construct understanding and significance from their experiences, transforming events into coherent narratives that inform identity, decision-making, and future action.

Educational resilience

The capacity to persist, adapt, and thrive academically despite encountering systemic barriers, institutional exclusions, and personal challenges. Unlike general resilience, educational resilience specifically encompasses the development of academic identity, learning strategies, and institutional navigation skills.

Identity reconstruction

The deliberate process of reformulating one's sense of self, moving beyond stigmatized or criminalized identities toward self-authored narratives that integrate past experiences with future aspirations.

Justice-impacted individuals

People who have experienced direct contact with the criminal justice system, including incarceration, community supervision, or other forms of correctional involvement. This term is preferred over "ex-offender" or "former criminal" as it centers the impact of systems rather than defining individuals by past actions.

Self-authorship

The capacity to internally define one's beliefs, identity, and relationships rather than relying on external authorities or social expectations. In post-incarceration contexts, self-authorship represents the reclamation of agency and the development of internally-generated life direction.

Critical resilience

A framework that reframes resilience not as individual adaptation to adverse circumstances, but as resistance to systems that create adversity in the first place. Critical resilience emphasizes collective action and systemic change rather than purely individual coping strategies.

Autoethnography

A qualitative research methodology that combines autobiographical writing with ethnographic inquiry, using personal experience as a lens for understanding broader cultural and social phenomena. This approach positions the researcher's lived experience as legitimate scholarly data.

Transformative learning

Mezirow's theory describing the process through which adults critically examine and revise their assumptions, beliefs, and perspectives, leading to fundamental shifts in understanding and behavior. This learning involves critical reflection, discourse, and action.

Systemic barriers

Institutional policies, practices, and cultural norms that systematically exclude or disadvantage formerly incarcerated individuals, including educational policies, employment restrictions, housing limitations, and social stigma embedded within organizational structures.

Phenomenological research

A qualitative methodology focused on understanding the lived experiences and essential meanings of phenomena as experienced by individuals, emphasizing the subjective, first-person perspective of participants.

Title and Rationale

Title: The Road to Redemption: An Autoethnographic and Phenomenological Study of Resilience and Higher Education in Post-Incarceration Reintegration

This title reflects the central themes of resilience, identity reconstruction, and the transformative role of higher education for formerly incarcerated individuals. The metaphor of "the road" acknowledges both the journey-based nature of reintegration and the ongoing process of meaning-making that characterizes post-incarceration life.

"Redemption" here is not positioned as a moral imperative requiring formerly incarcerated individuals to earn their place in society, but rather as a reclamation of agency and self-authorship through educational engagement.

The title prioritizes scholarly clarity over creative flair while signaling the multi-method approach grounded in qualitative inquiry that will drive this investigation. The coupling of autoethnography and phenomenology in the subtitle indicates the study's commitment to both personal narrative and broader experiential analysis.

This methodological pairing reflects the understanding that individual meaning-making occurs within larger social and systemic contexts, and that the researcher's positionality as a formerly incarcerated scholar adds both depth and complexity to the inquiry. The title thus establishes the study's theoretical foundation while positioning it within the broader landscape of transformative education research and critical reintegration studies.

Central Research Question

How do formerly incarcerated individuals make meaning of their higher education experiences as a means of navigating systemic barriers, fostering resilience, and reconstructing identity?

This question drives the study and invites layered responses that operate across multiple dimensions of experience and analysis. At its core, the question seeks to understand the interpretive processes through which formerly incarcerated individuals transform educational encounters into tools for personal and social navigation.

Agency-Centered Approach

The emphasis on "meaning-making" positions participants as active agents in their own transformation rather than passive recipients of educational intervention, aligning with critical pedagogical frameworks that privilege agency and self-determination.

Complex Recognition

The question's complexity lies in its recognition that higher education functions simultaneously as a site of opportunity and exclusion for formerly incarcerated individuals.

While educational attainment can provide practical benefits—employment prospects, social capital, cognitive skill development—the subjective experience of pursuing education while managing stigma, stereotypes, and systemic barriers requires deeper investigation. How individuals make meaning of these challenges and develop resilience through educational engagement varies significantly based on personal histories, identities, and interpretive frameworks.

Phenomenological Focus: The question allows for phenomenological exploration of how individuals internalize, resist, and reshape educational experiences to serve their reintegration goals, while also inviting autoethnographic reflection on the researcher's own parallel journey.

By foregrounding resilience and identity reconstruction, the question acknowledges that post-incarceration education is never merely academic but always involves fundamental questions of self-concept, belonging, and future possibility. This framing moves beyond traditional metrics of educational success to examine how learning becomes a vehicle for psychological healing, social reconnection, and narrative transformation in the lives of justice-impacted individuals.

Relationship to Previous Scholarship

Scholarship on education and reentry often focuses on recidivism rates or program effectiveness, neglecting how individuals internalize and interpret their educational journeys post-incarceration. This quantitative emphasis, while valuable for policy development, fails to capture the nuanced psychological and social processes that characterize educational transformation for justice-impacted populations.

Quantitative Focus Gap

Current research emphasizes measurable outcomes rather than subjective experience and meaning-making processes.

Researcher-Subject Distance

Traditional studies examine formerly incarcerated students as subjects of inquiry rather than co-constructors of knowledge.

Deficit-Based Approaches

Existing scholarship often focuses on barriers and deficits rather than assets, agency, and expertise of formerly incarcerated learners.

Limited Insider Perspectives

Few studies privilege narrative and subjective meaning-making or center the voices of formerly incarcerated scholars themselves.

Researchers like Honeywell (2018) and Runell (2021) have begun to examine self-transformation through higher education, but few studies privilege narrative and subjective meaning-making as primary data sources or center the voices of formerly incarcerated scholars themselves.

Study Contribution: This study addresses that gap by employing a multi-method qualitative design that centers lived experience—both my own and that of peers—to explore how resilience and identity evolve through educational engagement.

Theoretical Framework

This study is grounded in multiple theoretical frameworks that work together to provide a comprehensive lens for understanding educational meaning-making in post-incarceration contexts.

Resilience Theory
Luthar et al., 2000

Provides the conceptual framework for understanding how protective factors and adaptive processes operate in post-incarceration contexts.

Transformative Learning Theory
Mezirow, 1991

Offers crucial insights into how critical reflection and perspective transformation occur through educational engagement, particularly relevant for adults navigating major life transitions.

Critical Resilience
Bottrell, 2009

Reframes resilience not as individual adaptation to adverse circumstances, but as resistance to systems that create adversity in the first place.

Desistance Theory
Maruna, 2001

Examines narrative identity reconstruction processes that align closely with this study's focus on meaning-making and self-authorship.

Intersectionality
Crenshaw, 1989

Provides essential analytical tools for understanding how multiple marginalized identities compound educational barriers and inform resilience strategies.

Integration: This theoretical constellation moves beyond individual-focused resilience models to examine how educational resilience develops in response to structural inequalities. The integration of Critical Resilience Theory is particularly important, as it reframes resilience not as individual adaptation to adverse circumstances, but as resistance to systems that create adversity in the first place.

The study contributes to conversations across adult learning theory, critical criminology, and higher education reform by positioning education as simultaneously a site of healing and resistance, personal transformation and social critique.

Methodology

This dissertation employs a dual qualitative methodology, combining autoethnography with phenomenological research to create a comprehensive framework for understanding educational meaning-making in post-incarceration contexts.

Autoethnography (Lead Method)

Framework: Chang's (2008) systematic approach to autoethnographic inquiry

Data Collection: Systematic review of personal documents, culture grams to map social and institutional contexts, structured self-reflective writing exercises

Focus: My own journey through incarceration, reentry, and educational transformation

Phenomenological Research

Framework: Moustakas's (1994) transcendental phenomenology

Participants: 6-10 formerly incarcerated individuals pursuing or having completed higher education

Method: In-depth semi-structured interviews exploring lived experiences of educational engagement

Detailed Methodological Framework ▼

Chang's Autoethnographic Framework

Chang's emphasis on the connection between self, other, and culture provides the methodological foundation for transforming personal experience into rigorous scholarly analysis. Her framework ensures that the autoethnographic work moves beyond mere personal storytelling to examine how individual experiences reflect broader cultural and systemic dynamics affecting formerly incarcerated learners.

This study employs what Ellis, Adams, and Bochner (2011) classify as personal narrative autoethnography, writing evocative narratives specifically focused on my academic, research, and personal life as it intersects with post-incarceration educational experiences.

Phenomenological Interview Process

The phenomenological component will explore participants' lived experiences of educational engagement, focusing on:

  • Moments of meaning-making
  • Barrier navigation strategies
  • Identity reconstruction processes
  • Educational transformation experiences

Data Analysis Strategy

Data analysis will employ Saldaña's (2016) coding methods across both autoethnographic and phenomenological data:

  • In Vivo coding: Preserve participants' own language
  • Process coding: Capture dynamic meaning-making activities
  • Values coding: Identify underlying belief systems and motivations
The choice of autoethnography aligns with the study's commitment to disrupting traditional power dynamics in academic research, particularly those that position formerly incarcerated individuals as objects of study rather than knowledge producers.

As Ellis, Adams, and Bochner (2011) note, autoethnography treats "research as a political, socially-just and socially-conscious act" that challenges conventional ways of representing others. By foregrounding my own narrative through Chang's systematic approach, the methodology challenges deficit-based approaches to justice-impacted populations while modeling the kind of self-authorship that educational transformation can facilitate.

Methodological Strength: The combined methodology enables triangulation between self-narrative and peer insight, producing a textured understanding of how education functions as both personal catalyst and social intervention in post-incarceration contexts.

Methodological Considerations and Ethical Framework

Relational Ethics ▼

Following Ellis, Adams, and Bochner's (2011) emphasis on relational ethics, this study acknowledges that autoethnographic research inherently implicates others in the researcher's narrative. When I write about my educational journey, family members, mentors, fellow students, and institutional representatives become part of the story, even when not explicitly named.

Ethical Strategies

  1. Sharing relevant portions of the autoethnographic narrative with key individuals mentioned in my story
  2. Using protective measures such as altering identifying characteristics when necessary
  3. Being transparent about my dual role as researcher and subject
  4. Acknowledging that relationships may be affected by what I choose to reveal or analyze
Validity and Trustworthiness in Autoethnographic Research ▼

Traditional concepts of reliability, validity, and generalizability require reframing within autoethnographic methodology. As Ellis, Adams, and Bochner (2011) explain, autoethnographers value "narrative truth based on what a story of experience does—how it is used, understood, and responded to for and by us and others."

Validity Measures

  • Authenticity: Whether the experiences described feel lifelike, believable, and possible to readers
  • Credibility: Systematic documentation using Chang's methods
  • Triangulation: With phenomenological data from other participants
  • Transparency: About the subjective nature of memory and interpretation
Addressing Potential Criticisms ▼

Autoethnography faces common criticisms including concerns about academic rigor, accusations of "navel-gazing narcissism," and questions about analytical depth. This study addresses these concerns by:

  1. Employing Chang's systematic analytical framework that moves beyond personal storytelling
  2. Combining autoethnographic data with phenomenological interviews
  3. Grounding personal experience in established theoretical frameworks
  4. Using rigorous coding methods to identify patterns and themes

This foundational work in resilience theory provides the conceptual framework for understanding how protective factors and adaptive processes operate in post-incarceration contexts.

Mezirow's transformative learning theory offers crucial insights into how critical reflection and perspective transformation occur through educational engagement, particularly relevant for adults navigating major life transitions.

This seminal work in desistance theory examines narrative identity reconstruction processes that align closely with this study's focus on meaning-making and self-authorship.

Intersectionality theory provides essential analytical tools for understanding how multiple marginalized identities compound educational barriers and inform resilience strategies.

This study examines transformative learning processes among incarcerated students, providing important context for post-release educational experiences.

This work explores institutional barriers and support strategies for justice-impacted students, informing both theoretical framework and practical applications.
